# Real Estate Visual Knowledge Base

## VISUAL RED FLAGS (by severity)

### DEAL-KILLERS — always flag explicitly

**Foundation / Structural**
- Horizontal cracks in basement walls: structural failure, extremely serious
- Stair-step cracks in brick or concrete block: foundation movement
- Bowing or bulging basement walls: lateral soil pressure, urgent
- Cracks wider than ¼ inch anywhere on foundation: major concern
- Sagging or uneven roofline visible from exterior: load-bearing failure

**Water / Moisture**
- Efflorescence on basement walls: white or gray powdery mineral deposits = proof water is actively migrating through the foundation. Not cosmetic — it means there is a continuous moisture source. The white powder is salts left behind as water evaporates
- Dark staining on basement walls/floors especially in corners or along seams
- Visible mold (dark splotchy areas) on walls, ceilings, or around windows
- Fresh paint in isolated patches on basement walls or ceilings: classic cover-up signal — sellers paint over stains right before listing
- Water pooling or staining around foundation base in exterior photos

**Electrical**
- Fuse box (round glass fuses) instead of a modern breaker panel: pre-1960s, fire hazard, uninsurable with many carriers
- Rust, burn marks, or melted wiring at panel: active overloading
- Knob-and-tube wiring visible in attic or open basement ceiling: ceramic white knobs mounted to joists with cloth-wrapped wire running between them. Pre-1940. Extremely dangerous, many insurers refuse coverage entirely

**Plumbing**
- Polybutylene pipes: flexible plastic tubing in gray, white, or orange. Common in homes built 1975–1998. Prone to microscopic cracking from chlorine/oxidation. Many insurers refuse coverage. Class action settlements happened in the 1990s. Look near water heater connections and under vanity sinks
- Galvanized steel pipes with visible orange/brown rust and corrosion buildup: restricted flow, leaks imminent

---

### SIGNIFICANT — always mention

- Single-pane windows: visible gap between frame and one sheet of glass, no interior frosting on cold days. Significant energy loss. Common pre-1980
- Bathroom tile grout cracking at tub or shower surround: water intrusion pathway behind the wall
- Rotting fascia board (the horizontal trim beneath the roofline): indicates gutter overflow and water saturation; signals deferred exterior maintenance
- Gutters missing, sagging, or pulling away from fascia
- Negative grading: ground slopes toward the foundation rather than away from it — directs every rain event toward the basement
- Outdated HVAC unit visible (rust-streaked, very old cabinet style, pre-2000 aesthetic): budget $8,000–$15,000 for replacement
- Popcorn ceilings in homes built before 1980: potential asbestos. Do not disturb without testing

---

### MINOR / ASK-ABOUT

- Patchy or mismatched drywall repairs: often cover past water damage or pest entry points
- Mismatched flooring between adjacent rooms: signals renovation work of varying quality
- Drop ceilings (suspended tile grid) in basement: almost always hiding something — low clearance, leaking pipes, or old wiring
- Visible condensation or fogging between double-pane window glass: broken seal, moisture inside

## HIDDEN VALUE SIGNALS

- **Original hardwood floors**: visible in photos or obvious carpet seams/bumps/nail patterns showing hardwood underneath. Refinishing returns 147% on investment. Signals solid original construction
- **Period architectural details intact**: ornate crown moldings, plaster ceiling medallions, original built-ins, wainscoting, transom windows in Victorian/Federal/Italianate homes. These are expensive to recreate and add authentic character value
- **Large unfinished basement with adequate ceiling height** (visible ceiling clearance of 7+ feet): potential to add 400–800 sqft of living space for relatively low cost compared to above-grade addition
- **Oversized or corner lot visible in aerial or street-view shots**: expansion potential, ADU opportunity, privacy — rare in denser Cincinnati neighborhoods
- **Detached structure with good bones**: separate garage, carriage house, or workshop. Even cosmetically poor, a structurally sound detached structure adds significant value
- **Evidence of recent major system upgrades**: new HVAC cabinet, updated electrical panel (visible through open door), newer water heater date sticker, new roof shingles
- **South-facing backyard** (visible in exterior shots by sun angle): maximizes sunlight, better for gardens, more desirable
- **Solid brick construction**: more durable than wood frame, lower insurance, better noise isolation

## ROOM-BY-ROOM VISUAL CHECKLIST

**Kitchen**
- Cabinet bottoms showing swelling, warping, or water stains = under-sink leak
- Countertop cracks, chips, or visible seams at backsplash = water entry
- Appliance age (look for control panel style, brand logos, handle design)
- Ventilation hood present and appropriately sized to cooktop
- Outlet placement (GFCI required near sink — look for the TEST/RESET button)
- Floor condition near dishwasher (soft spots = past leak)

**Bathrooms**
- Grout integrity at tub and shower surround: cracking or missing grout = water behind wall
- Caulk condition at tub/wall/floor junctions: gaps = water entry
- Ventilation fan visible (absence = moisture issues, mold risk)
- Vanity cabinet floor: check for swelling or staining under sink
- Tile lippage (unevenness at tile edges): signals poor installation or movement
- Toilet rocking or gap at floor: wax ring failure, possible subfloor damage

**Basement**
- Wall crack patterns and width (see foundation section above)
- Efflorescence presence and extent (see moisture section)
- Sump pump visible: presence is normal in Cincinnati; absence in a damp basement is a red flag
- Ceiling height (visible clearance against utility pipes)
- Water heater: look for rust on base, age sticker
- Furnace/HVAC unit: note brand, rust level, age if visible
- Structural beams/joists: sagging, dark discoloration (rot), or insect damage

**Exterior**
- Foundation visible along grade line: crack patterns, efflorescence
- Grading: does ground slope away from house or toward it
- Gutters: present, attached, clean, properly sloped
- Downspouts: extensions directing water at least 4–6 feet from foundation
- Siding material and condition: wood rot, paint peeling, gaps at seams
- Roof: visible from ground — look for missing shingles, moss, sagging ridge

**Living Areas**
- Ceiling stains (yellow/brown rings): past or active roof or plumbing leak from above
- Window condition and type (single vs. double pane)
- Door and window alignment: stuck doors, gaps at frame = structural settling
- Floor material: hardwood vs. carpet over hardwood vs. vinyl
- Baseboard and trim condition: gaps at floor = settling or moisture movement
