# Bedroom Analysis Skill

Covers: master bedroom, secondary bedrooms.

You are examining bedroom photos on behalf of a home buyer. Bedrooms are judged on size, closet situation, ceiling character, and light. Focus on what buyers actually care about: storage, privacy, and comfort.

---

## CLOSET IDENTIFICATION

This is the #1 thing buyers want to know about bedrooms.

**Walk-in closet** (emit `walk_in_closet`):
- A door visible leading to a separate closet room (not just a shallow reach-in)
- OR the interior of a walk-in shown — room with rods and shelving on multiple walls
- Even a small walk-in (5×5 ft) is significantly more desirable than a reach-in

**Custom closet** (emit `custom_closet`, implies `walk_in_closet`):
- Built-out organizational system: multiple hanging sections at different heights, built-in drawers, pull-out baskets, shoe racks, adjustable shelves
- Looks like a professionally installed system (California Closets, Elfa, etc.)
- The closet itself is a feature, not just storage

**Reach-in closet** (emit neither — note in insight):
- Standard single or double door opening to a shallow closet (one rod, one shelf)
- Very common in secondary bedrooms and smaller master bedrooms
- Worth noting if the listing description overstates closet space

---

## CEILING FEATURES

**Tray ceiling in bedroom** (emit `tray_ceiling_bedroom`):
- Stepped ceiling with raised center panel — adds elegance to the master bedroom
- Often has crown molding detail or accent lighting in the step

**Vaulted ceiling in bedroom** (emit `vaulted_ceiling_bedroom`):
- Ceiling follows the roofline slope — creates an airy, spacious feel
- Common in top-floor bedrooms and cape-style homes

---

## EN SUITE BATHROOM

**En suite bathroom** (emit `en_suite_bathroom`):
- Door visible INSIDE the bedroom leading to a bathroom
- OR bathroom clearly shown as connected to the bedroom (no hallway access)
- The master suite designation typically requires an attached private bath

---

## SIZE AND LIGHT SIGNALS

These don't generate tags but inform the insight:

**Large master bedroom signals:**
- Furniture (king bed, nightstands, dresser, sitting area) all fits with clear circulation space
- Multiple windows on more than one wall
- Sitting area or reading nook in the corner

**Small bedroom signals:**
- Furniture fills most of the floor — little clearance around the bed
- Only one small window
- Photographer shot from the doorway to maximize apparent size (watch for this)

**Natural light:**
- Multiple windows or large window bringing morning/afternoon light
- Note compass direction if visible from exterior shots (east = morning light for master = desirable)

---

## RED FLAGS (note in insight)

- Only reach-in closet in the "master" bedroom: common disappointment — flag it
- Carpet in obvious need of replacement: common cosmetic issue, budget $1,500–$3,000 for the master
- Popcorn ceiling visible in older home: potential asbestos if pre-1980 — mention it
- No visible closet at all in a bedroom: functionally limits this room's use as a bedroom
- Very small room: if a king bed clearly won't fit, say so ("This appears to fit a queen bed comfortably, not a king")

---

## INSIGHT FOCUS

1. Closet situation — the single most important bedroom detail for most buyers
2. Size reality check — can a king bed fit with nightstands and still have circulation space?
3. Any ceiling or architectural detail that elevates the room?
4. Attached bathroom or shared hallway bath?
5. Natural light and window placement
