# Buyer Profile Framework

## HOW TO DETECT THE BUYER PROFILE

Use price, beds, and listing signals to infer the most likely buyer and calibrate your analysis accordingly.

---

## PROFILES

### First-Time Buyer
**Signals**: Price under $350,000 / 2–3 bedrooms / typical starter home footprint

**What they need most:**
- Move-in readiness assessment: can they actually live here on day one without major work?
- Clear breakdown of what is urgent vs. what can wait
- Budget estimates they can plan around (not vague "may need repairs")
- Explanation of real estate terms they may not know (efflorescence, polybutylene, knob-and-tube)
- Protection from hidden major costs that could overwhelm a tight budget

**Tone:** Protective, thorough, plain-language. Explain what things mean. Err on the side of caution. A $12,000 HVAC surprise can be a financial catastrophe for a first-time buyer — say so clearly.

**What NOT to emphasize:** Minor cosmetic issues, personal style preferences, luxury finishing comparisons

---

### Move-Up / Family Buyer
**Signals**: $350,000–$700,000 / 3–4 bedrooms / suburban location / school district mention

**What they need most:**
- Livability for children: safety, practicality, layout
- Outdoor space assessment: yard, fencing, pool safety, garage access
- Storage sufficiency: closets, garage size, basement
- Schools and neighborhood stability (not always visible in photos, but note if listing mentions it)
- Big-ticket items that would disrupt family life: HVAC, roof, major plumbing
- Systems that are recent vs. due for replacement within 5 years

**Tone:** Practical, safety-conscious, day-1 livability focus. Think: "Can my family actually live here comfortably?" Mention child safety considerations (pool fencing, deck railings, stair rails visible in photos).

**What NOT to emphasize:** Ultra-luxury differentiation, investor ROI framing

---

### Luxury Buyer
**Signals**: Price $700,000+ / premium location / high sqft / words like "estate," "custom," "designer"

**What they need most:**
- Finish quality and craftsmanship assessment (use the luxury.md knowledge)
- Consistency of quality throughout — not just a showcase kitchen with builder-grade bathrooms
- Uniqueness and character: what makes this home special vs. comparable luxury homes
- Anything that undermines the premium positioning: dated secondary spaces, inconsistent quality, staging masking mediocre finishes
- Red flags that are particularly costly at this level: deferred maintenance on a $1.2M home is inexcusable

**Tone:** Discerning and elevated. They are buying a lifestyle and expect the home to deliver it. Be direct about where the finish quality delivers and where it falls short. Acknowledge genuine craftsmanship. Call out luxury brands and materials you recognize. Flag anything that feels "almost luxury but not quite."

**What NOT to emphasize:** Basic budget advice, explaining what countertops are

---

### Investor / Flipper
**Signals**: Multi-family / high days-on-market / estate sale / price significantly below area comps / "as-is" condition / price per sqft far below market

**What they need most:**
- Structural soundness vs. cosmetic scope: they accept cosmetic work, need to know about bones
- Cost estimate for stabilization: what does it take to bring this property to rentable or sellable condition?
- Upside identification: what features will appeal to future buyers or renters?
- Red flags that kill ROI: polybutylene pipes ($8-15k), knob-and-tube rewire ($15-40k), foundation work ($10-30k), environmental issues
- Rental potential indicators: extra bedroom, separate entrance, good location fundamentals

**Tone:** Terse, numbers-focused. Frames everything as scope and cost. Does not over-explain. Treats them as a professional making a business decision. Lead with: here is what is broken, here is roughly what it costs, here is the upside.

**What NOT to emphasize:** Cosmetic concerns, staging quality, personal preference items

---

### Renovator / Gut-Rehab Buyer
**Signals**: Explicitly "investor special," "estate condition," "bring your vision," very low price/sqft, photos showing significant deferred maintenance

**What they need most:**
- Structural bones assessment: is the foundation solid? Are the walls straight? Is the framing sound?
- Scope clarity: cosmetic-only (paint, floors, fixtures) vs. structural vs. systems
- What can be kept vs. what must be replaced
- Major scope items and rough order-of-magnitude cost
- Any environmental/regulatory concerns that affect permit-ability (lead paint abatement, asbestos, flood zone)

**Tone:** Contractor-speak. No hand-holding on cosmetic items — they know paint is cosmetic. Focus entirely on the structural and systems story. Use terms like "gut to studs," "systems replacement," "envelope integrity," "permit-ready scope."

**What NOT to emphasize:** Staging, finish quality, luxury comparisons, minor cosmetic issues
